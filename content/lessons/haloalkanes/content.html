Introduction to Haloalkanes

Haloalkanes (alkyl halides) are organic compounds containing one or more halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I) attached to an aliphatic carbon chain.

General Formula

R-X where R = alkyl group, X = F, Cl, Br, or I

Importance in Organic Chemistry

Haloalkanes → Versatile intermediates → Various compounds
Role: Starting materials for numerous synthetic transformations

Classification of Haloalkanes

Based on Carbon Atom

Primary, Secondary & Tertiary Haloalkanes

  • Primary (1°): Halogen attached to primary carbon (CH₃-CH₂-X)
  • Secondary (2°): Halogen attached to secondary carbon
  • Tertiary (3°): Halogen attached to tertiary carbon
  • Allylic: Halogen on carbon adjacent to C=C
  • Benzylic: Halogen on carbon adjacent to benzene ring

Based on Number of Halogen Atoms

Mono-, Di-, Tri-, and Polyhaloalkanes

  • Monohaloalkanes: One halogen (e.g., CH₃Cl)
  • Dihaloalkanes: Two halogens (e.g., CH₂Cl₂)
  • Trihaloalkanes: Three halogens (e.g., CHCl₃)
  • Polyhaloalkanes: Multiple halogens (e.g., CCl₄)

Special Compounds

Geminal vs Vicinal

Gem-dihalides: Both halogens on same carbon (CH₃-CHCl₂)
Vic-dihalides: Halogens on adjacent carbons (CH₂Cl-CH₂Cl)
Alkylidene dihalides: Both halogens on same carbon of alkene

Nomenclature of Haloalkanes

IUPAC Rules

1. Name parent alkane (longest carbon chain with halogen)
2. Halogen as prefix (fluoro, chloro, bromo, iodo)
3. Number chain to give halogen lowest number
4. For multiple halogens: use di-, tri-, tetra- prefixes

Examples

CH₃-CH₂-Cl → Chloroethane
CH₃-CHCl-CH₃ → 2-Chloropropane
CH₂Cl-CH₂Cl → 1,2-Dichloroethane
CH₃-CCl₃ → 1,1,1-Trichloroethane
Br-CH₂-CH=CH₂ → 3-Bromoprop-1-ene (Allyl bromide)
Common vs IUPAC Names

Common names are still widely used: methyl chloride (chloromethane), ethyl bromide (bromomethane), isopropyl iodide (2-iodopropane).

Physical Properties

Characteristic Properties

  • State: Methyl/ethyl chlorides are gases; C₃-C₁₈ are liquids; higher are solids
  • Odor: Sweet smell (chloroform), heavy (bromoform), unpleasant (iodoform)
  • Boiling Points: RI > RBr > RCl > RF > RH (increases with molecular weight)
  • Density: Bromo and iodoalkanes are heavier than water (d > 1)
  • Solubility: Insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents
  • Dipole Moment: C-X bond is polar (Cδ⁺-Xδ⁻)

Boiling Point Trends

Compound Boiling Point (°C) Trend
CH₃-F -78 Lowest
CH₃-Cl -24 Intermediate
CH₃-Br 4 Higher
CH₃-I 43 Highest
Bond Strength and Reactivity

C-F bond is strongest (485 kJ/mol) and least reactive.
C-I bond is weakest (228 kJ/mol) and most reactive.
Reactivity order: RI > RBr > RCl > RF

Preparation Methods

From Alcohols

Reaction with Halogen Acids

R-OH + HX → R-X + H₂O
Reactivity: HI > HBr > HCl (HF doesn't react directly)
Mechanism: SN1 for 3° alcohols, SN2 for 1° alcohols

Lucas Test

R-OH + HCl/ZnCl₂ → R-Cl (cloudiness indicates reaction)
Observation: 3° alcohols react immediately, 2° in 5 min, 1° no reaction

From Alkenes

Addition of Hydrogen Halides

CH₂=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃-CH₂-Br
Mechanism: Electrophilic addition (Markovnikov's rule)

Anti-Markovnikov Addition (Peroxide Effect)

CH₃-CH=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-Br
Conditions: Presence of peroxides (ROOR), free radical mechanism

From Alkanes

Free Radical Halogenation

CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃-Cl + HCl
Conditions: UV light or heat, radical chain mechanism
Selectivity: 3° > 2° > 1° H (relative reactivity 5:3.8:1 for Cl₂)

Halogen Exchange (Finkelstein)

Preparation of Iodoalkanes

R-Cl + NaI → R-I + NaCl
Conditions: Acetone solvent (NaCl insoluble, drives reaction)

Hunsdiecker Reaction

Decarboxylative Halogenation

R-COOAg + Br₂ → R-Br + CO₂ + AgBr
Conditions: Silver carboxylate with bromine in CCl₄

From Ethers

Cleavage with HI

R-O-R' + 2HI → R-I + R'-I + H₂O
Note: Both alkyl groups converted to iodides

Chemical Reactions

Reactivity Pattern

Haloalkanes are highly reactive due to polar C-X bond. Carbon bears partial positive charge, making it susceptible to nucleophilic attack.

Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

Factors Affecting SN1 vs SN2

  • Substrate: 3° favor SN1, 1° favor SN2, 2° can go either way
  • Nucleophile: Strong bases favor SN2, weak bases favor SN1
  • Solvent: Polar protic favors SN1, polar aprotic favors SN2
  • Leaving group: Better leaving groups increase rate (I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻)

SN2 Mechanism (Bimolecular)

Characteristics of SN2

Nu⁻ + R-X → [Nu···R···X]⁻ → Nu-R + X⁻
Features: One step, inversion of configuration, 2nd order kinetics
Steric hindrance: Decreases rate (CH₃X > 1° > 2° > 3°)

SN1 Mechanism (Unimolecular)

Characteristics of SN1

R-X ⇌ R⁺ + X⁻ (slow) → R⁺ + Nu⁻ → R-Nu (fast)
Features: Two steps, carbocation intermediate, racemization, 1st order kinetics
Stability: 3° > 2° > 1° carbocations (methyl doesn't undergo SN1)

Common Nucleophilic Substitutions

With Hydroxide Ion (Alcohols)

R-X + OH⁻ → R-OH + X⁻
Conditions: Aqueous NaOH/KOH, hydrolysis

With Cyanide Ion (Nitriles)

R-X + CN⁻ → R-CN + X⁻
Importance: Increases carbon chain length by one

With Ammonia (Amines)

R-X + NH₃ → R-NH₂ + HX → R-NH₃⁺X⁻
Note: Can give primary, secondary, tertiary amines, and quaternary salts

Elimination Reactions

E2 vs E1

E2: One step, strong base required, anti-periplanar geometry, Zaitsev rule
E1: Two steps, weak base, carbocation intermediate, mixture of alkenes

Dehydrohalogenation

CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-Br + OH⁻ → CH₃-CH=CH₂ + Br⁻ + H₂O
Conditions: Alcoholic KOH, heat
Saytzeff Rule: More substituted alkene is major product

Reduction to Alkanes

With Zinc and Acid

R-X + Zn + H⁺ → R-H + Zn²⁺ + X⁻
Alternative: LiAlH₄, NaBH₄, or catalytic hydrogenation

Wurtz Reaction

Coupling Reaction

2R-X + 2Na → R-R + 2NaX
Conditions: Dry ether, sodium metal
Limitation: Gives mixture with unsymmetrical halides

Formation of Grignard Reagents

Organomagnesium Compounds

R-X + Mg → R-Mg-X
Conditions: Dry ether, iodine crystal to initiate
Importance: Versatile reagents for C-C bond formation

Comparison: SN1 vs SN2

Feature SN1 SN2
Molecularity Unimolecular Bimolecular
Rate Law Rate = k[RX] Rate = k[RX][Nu]
Stereochemistry Racemization Inversion
Rearrangement Possible Not possible
Best Substrate 3° > 2° 1° > 2° > 3°

Applications of Haloalkanes

Industrial and Laboratory Uses

  • Solvents: CHCl₃, CCl₄, CH₂Cl₂ (degreasing, dry cleaning)
  • Refrigerants: CFCs (now phased out), HCFCs, HFCs
  • Fire Extinguishers: CBr₂F₂ (halon), now restricted
  • Anesthetics: Chloroform, halothane (CF₃-CHClBr)
  • Pesticides: DDT, BHC, aldrin, dieldrin (many now banned)
  • Pharmaceuticals: Synthetic intermediates
  • Plastics: Vinyl chloride (PVC), tetrafluoroethylene (Teflon)

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs)

CFC → UV light → Cl• → O₃ → O₂ + ClO•
Problem: Ozone layer depletion (Montreal Protocol, 1987)
Alternatives: HCFCs (less harmful), HFCs (no chlorine)
Environmental Concerns

Many haloalkanes are persistent in the environment, bioaccumulate in fatty tissues, and some are carcinogenic (vinyl chloride, carbon tetrachloride). Proper disposal and alternatives are essential.

Important Commercial Haloalkanes

Chloroform (CHCl₃): Former anesthetic, now solvent
Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄): Former fire extinguisher, now banned
Methylene chloride (CH₂Cl₂): Paint stripper, degreaser
Freon-12 (CCl₂F₂): Refrigerant, now phased out
Vinyl chloride (CH₂=CHCl): Monomer for PVC
Teflon (CF₂=CF₂ polymer): Non-stick coatings

Synthesis of Important Compounds

CH₃Cl → CH₃OH → HCHO (formaldehyde)
C₂H₅Cl → C₂H₅CN → C₂H₅COOH (propionic acid)
C₆H₅-CH₂Cl → C₆H₅-CH₂CN → C₆H₅-CH₂COOH (phenylacetic acid)
Importance: Key intermediates in chemical industry

Safety Precautions

  • Toxicity: Many are toxic (liver damage, carcinogenic)
  • Flammability: Some are flammable (methyl chloride, ethyl bromide)
  • Storage: Keep away from light (iodoalkanes decompose)
  • Disposal: Never pour down drain, use proper waste disposal
  • Ventilation: Always use in fume hood
