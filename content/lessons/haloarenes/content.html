Introduction to Haloarenes

Haloarenes (aryl halides) are aromatic compounds in which one or more hydrogen atoms of an aromatic ring are replaced by halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I).

Key Characteristic

Unlike haloalkanes, haloarenes have the halogen attached to an sp² hybridized carbon of an aromatic ring, making them less reactive in nucleophilic substitution.

General Structure

C₆H₅-X (where X = F, Cl, Br, I)
Example: Chlorobenzene (C₆H₅-Cl)

Classification of Haloarenes

Based on Type of Halogen

Fluoro, Chloro, Bromo, and Iodoarenes

  • Fluoroarenes: C₆H₅-F (fluorobenzene) - Most stable, strongest C-F bond
  • Chloroarenes: C₆H₅-Cl (chlorobenzene) - Most common industrially
  • Bromoarenes: C₆H₅-Br (bromobenzene) - Used in synthesis
  • Iodoarenes: C₆H₅-I (iodobenzene) - Most reactive, light-sensitive

Based on Number of Halogen Atoms

Mono-, Di-, and Polyhaloarenes

  • Monohaloarenes: One halogen (e.g., chlorobenzene)
  • Dihaloarenes: Two halogens (e.g., o-dichlorobenzene)
  • Trihaloarenes: Three halogens (e.g., 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene)
  • Polyhaloarenes: Multiple halogens (e.g., hexachlorobenzene)

Based on Relative Position

Isomerism in Dihaloarenes

Ortho (1,2), Meta (1,3), and Para (1,4) isomers have different physical properties and reactivity.

Nomenclature of Haloarenes

IUPAC Rules

1. Halogen is treated as a substituent (fluoro, chloro, bromo, iodo)
2. Number the ring to give lowest possible numbers
3. For multiple substituents, use di-, tri-, tetra- prefixes
4. Follow alphabetical order when naming

Examples

C₆H₅-Cl → Chlorobenzene
Cl-C₆H₄-Cl (1,2) → 1,2-Dichlorobenzene (o-dichlorobenzene)
Br-C₆H₄-NO₂ (1-bromo-4-nitro) → 1-Bromo-4-nitrobenzene
Cl₃-C₆H₃ (1,2,4) → 1,2,4-Trichlorobenzene
Common vs IUPAC Names

Common names are often used for simple haloarenes (chlorobenzene, bromobenzene), while IUPAC names are used for complex substituted compounds.

Physical Properties

Characteristic Properties

  • State: Colorless liquids (lower) or solids (higher members)
  • Odor: Pleasant almond-like (chlorobenzene), heavier for bromo/iodo
  • Boiling Points: Higher than haloalkanes due to planar structure
  • Density: Heavier than water (d > 1 g/mL)
  • Solubility: Insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents
  • Dipole Moment: Low due to resonance (C₆H₅-Cl: 1.69 D)

Boiling Point Comparison

Compound Boiling Point (°C)
Chlorobenzene 132
Bromobenzene 156
Iodobenzene 188
Resonance Effect

The C-X bond in haloarenes has partial double bond character due to resonance, making it shorter and stronger than in haloalkanes.

Preparation Methods

Direct Halogenation of Arenes

Chlorination of Benzene

C₆H₆ + Cl₂ → C₆H₅-Cl + HCl
Conditions: Lewis acid catalyst (FeCl₃, AlCl₃), dark, 50-55°C
Mechanism: Electrophilic aromatic substitution
Halogen Carriers

Lewis acids (FeX₃, AlX₃) polarize the halogen molecule, generating the electrophile X⁺ needed for aromatic substitution.

Sandmeyer Reaction

From Diazonium Salts

Ar-N₂⁺Cl⁻ + CuX → Ar-X + N₂ + CuCl
Conditions: Cuprous halide (CuX) in corresponding halogen acid
Scope: Excellent for Cl, Br, CN; poor for F, I

Gattermann Reaction

Alternative to Sandmeyer

Ar-N₂⁺Cl⁻ + Cu powder + HX → Ar-X + N₂
Conditions: Copper powder with halogen acid, milder conditions

Halex Process (Industrial)

Nucleophilic Halogen Exchange

Ar-Cl + KF → Ar-F + KCl
Conditions: High temperature (400-500°C), polar aprotic solvent
Application: Production of fluoroarenes from chloroarenes

From Phenols

Via Diazonium Intermediates

Ar-OH → Ar-N₂⁺ → Ar-X
Steps: Diazotization of amine from phenol reduction

Ullmann Reaction

Biaryl Synthesis

2Ar-I + Cu → Ar-Ar + CuI₂
Conditions: Copper bronze, high temperature, DMF or nitrobenzene

Chemical Reactions

Reactivity Difference

Haloarenes are less reactive than haloalkanes in nucleophilic substitution due to resonance stabilization and sp² hybridization.

Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

Why Difficult?

1. Resonance gives C-X bond partial double bond character
2. sp² hybridization makes carbon less accessible
3. Phenyl cation unstable if formed
4. Electron-rich nucleophiles repelled by electron-rich ring

Nucleophilic Aromatic Substitution (S_NAr)

Addition-Elimination Mechanism

Ar-X + Nu⁻ → [Ar-Nu-X]⁻ → Ar-Nu + X⁻
Requirements: Strong electron-withdrawing groups ortho/para to X
Example: 2,4-Dinitrochlorobenzene reacts easily with OH⁻

Benzyne Mechanism

Elimination-Addition via Benzyne

Ar-X + NH₂⁻ → Ar-NH₂ + X⁻
Conditions: Strong base (NaNH₂), liquid NH₃, high temperature
Intermediate: Highly reactive benzyne (C₆H₄)

Electrophilic Substitution

Orientation Effect

The halogen is ortho/para directing but deactivating due to -I effect dominating over +R effect.

Nitration

Formation of Nitrohaloarenes

C₆H₅-Cl + HNO₃ → o-Nitrochlorobenzene + p-Nitrochlorobenzene
Conditions: Conc. HNO₃ + conc. H₂SO₄, 50-55°C
Ratio: ~30% ortho, ~70% para (minor meta)

Sulfonation

Sulfonation of Chlorobenzene

C₆H₅-Cl + H₂SO₄ → Chlorobenzenesulfonic acids
Note: High temperature favors para product (thermodynamic control)

Wurtz-Fittig Reaction

Coupling with Alkyl Halides

Ar-X + R-X + 2Na → Ar-R + 2NaX
Conditions: Dry ether, sodium metal
Application: Synthesis of alkylbenzenes

Fittig Reaction

Symmetrical Biaryl Formation

2Ar-X + 2Na → Ar-Ar + 2NaX
Conditions: Similar to Wurtz but with two aryl halides

Formation of Grignard Reagents

Aryl Magnesium Halides

Ar-X + Mg → Ar-Mg-X
Conditions: Dry ether, reflux (iodo > bromo > chloro)
Note: Chloroarenes require activated magnesium or THF

Reduction Reactions

To Hydrocarbons

Ar-X + 2[H] → Ar-H + HX
Reducing agents: Zn/HCl, Ni/H₂, Pd/C/H₂

Comparison: Haloalkanes vs Haloarenes

  • Nucleophilic Substitution: Haloalkanes (easy, SN1/SN2) vs Haloarenes (difficult, requires activating groups)
  • C-X Bond Length: Shorter in haloarenes due to resonance
  • Dipole Moment: Lower in haloarenes
  • Reactivity Order: RI > RBr > RCl > RF (alkanes) vs ArI > ArBr > ArCl > ArF (arenes)

Applications of Haloarenes

Industrial and Pharmaceutical Uses

  • Solvents: Chlorobenzene (paint remover), o-dichlorobenzene
  • Insecticides: DDT, BHC (benzene hexachloride)
    • Pharmaceuticals: Chloramphenicol, Halothane (anesthetic)
  • Dyes: Intermediate in dye synthesis
  • Polymers: PVC plasticizers, flame retardants
  • Heat Transfer Fluids: Dichlorobenzenes, biphenyl mixtures

DDT (Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane)

(Cl-C₆H₄)₂CH-CCl₃
History: First synthetic insecticide (1939), Nobel Prize 1948
Issue: Environmental persistence led to ban in many countries
Environmental Concerns

Many haloarenes are persistent organic pollutants (POPs). They bioaccumulate in food chains, resist degradation, and can cause health issues (carcinogenic, endocrine disruptors). The Stockholm Convention regulates many chlorinated arenes.

Important Compounds

Chlorobenzene: Largest production volume, precursor to phenol, aniline
p-Dichlorobenzene: Mothballs, air fresheners
Hexachlorobenzene: Fungicide (now banned in most countries)
Polychlorinated Biphenyls (PCBs): Electrical insulators (banned)
2,4-D and 2,4,5-T: Herbicides (Agent Orange component)

Synthesis of Pharmaceuticals

Chlorobenzene → Phenol → Aspirin
p-Nitrochlorobenzene → p-Nitrophenol → Paracetamol
Importance: Key intermediates in drug manufacturing
