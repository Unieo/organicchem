Introduction to Phenols

Phenols are aromatic compounds containing one or more hydroxyl (-OH) groups directly attached to a benzene ring.

Key Difference from Alcohols

In phenols, the -OH group is attached to an sp² hybridized carbon of an aromatic ring, making them more acidic than alcohols.

General Structure

C₆H₅-OH (Phenol)
IUPAC Name: Hydroxybenzene

Classification of Phenols

Based on Number of -OH Groups

Types of Phenols

  • Monohydric phenols: One -OH group (e.g., Phenol, Cresols)
  • Dihydric phenols: Two -OH groups (e.g., Catechol, Resorcinol, Hydroquinone)
  • Trihydric phenols: Three -OH groups (e.g., Pyrogallol, Phloroglucinol)
  • Polyhydric phenols: Multiple -OH groups

Based on Substituents

Common Substituted Phenols

  • CH₃-C₆H₄-OH → Cresols (Methyl phenols)
  • Cl-C₆H₄-OH → Chlorophenols
  • NO₂-C₆H₄-OH → Nitrophenols

Nomenclature of Phenols

IUPAC Rules

1. Phenol is the parent name when -OH is the principal functional group
2. Number the ring to give -OH group the lowest possible number (position 1)
3. Other substituents are named as prefixes with appropriate locants

Examples

C₆H₅-OH → Phenol (common name: Carbolic acid)
CH₃-C₆H₄-OH (ortho) → 2-Methylphenol (o-Cresol)
Cl-C₆H₄-OH (meta) → 3-Chlorophenol (m-Chlorophenol)
OH-C₆H₄-OH (1,2) → Benzene-1,2-diol (Catechol)
Historical Note

Joseph Lister first used phenol as an antiseptic in surgery in 1867, revolutionizing medical practice.

Physical Properties

Characteristic Properties

  • State: Colorless crystals or liquids with characteristic "hospital smell"
  • Melting Point: Phenol: 40.5°C (can cause severe burns)
  • Boiling Point: Phenol: 181.7°C
  • Solubility: Partially soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding
  • Acidity: pKa ≈ 10 (more acidic than alcohols, less than carboxylic acids)

Hydrogen Bonding in Phenols

Ph-OH···O-H-Ph (Intermolecular)
Effect: Higher boiling points than comparable hydrocarbons
Safety Warning

Phenol is corrosive and toxic. It can be absorbed through skin causing systemic toxicity. Always use gloves and proper ventilation.

Preparation Methods

Cumene Process (Industrial)

Cumene Hydroperoxide Rearrangement

C₆H₅-CH(CH₃)₂ + O₂ → C₆H₅-C(CH₃)₂-O-OH
C₆H₅-C(CH₃)₂-O-OH + H⁺ → C₆H₅-OH + CH₃COCH₃
Conditions: Air oxidation of cumene, acid-catalyzed rearrangement
Importance

The cumene process produces both phenol and acetone, making it economically important. About 95% of industrial phenol is made this way.

Dow Process

Chlorobenzene Hydrolysis

C₆H₅-Cl + 2NaOH → C₆H₅-ONa + NaCl + H₂O
C₆H₅-ONa + HCl → C₆H₅-OH + NaCl
Conditions: High temperature (350°C), high pressure, copper catalyst

Sulfonation Process

Benzene Sulfonation & Fusion

C₆H₆ + H₂SO₄ → C₆H₅-SO₃H + H₂O
C₆H₅-SO₃H + 3NaOH → C₆H₅-ONa + Na₂SO₃ + 2H₂O
Conditions: Fuming H₂SO₄, then fusion with NaOH at 300°C

Chemical Reactions

Acidity and Salt Formation

Reaction with Sodium Hydroxide

C₆H₅-OH + NaOH → C₆H₅-O⁻Na⁺ + H₂O
Observation: Phenol dissolves in NaOH but not in NaHCO₃ (distinguishes from carboxylic acids)
Why Phenols are Acidic

The phenoxide ion is stabilized by resonance with the aromatic ring, making deprotonation easier than with alcohols.

Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution

Activating Effect

The -OH group is strongly activating and ortho/para directing due to resonance donation of electrons to the ring.

Nitration

Formation of Nitrophenols

C₆H₅-OH + HNO₃ (dilute) → o-Nitrophenol + p-Nitrophenol
C₆H₅-OH + HNO₃ (conc.) → 2,4,6-Trinitrophenol (Picric acid)
Conditions: Dilute HNO₃ at room temperature gives mono-nitration

Halogenation

Bromination of Phenol

C₆H₅-OH + 3Br₂ → 2,4,6-Tribromophenol + 3HBr
Conditions: Aqueous bromine at room temperature
Observation: White precipitate forms immediately (test for phenol)

Sulfonation

Sulfonation of Phenol

C₆H₅-OH + H₂SO₄ → o-Hydroxybenzenesulfonic acid (low temp)
C₆H₅-OH + H₂SO₄ → p-Hydroxybenzenesulfonic acid (high temp)
Note: Temperature controls orientation (kinetic vs thermodynamic control)

Friedel-Crafts Reactions

Alkylation and Acylation

C₆H₅-OH + (CH₃CO)₂O → C₆H₅-O-CO-CH₃ (Phenyl acetate)
Conditions: Requires mild conditions due to high reactivity

Esterification

Formation of Esters

C₆H₅-OH + (CH₃CO)₂O → C₆H₅-O-CO-CH₃ + CH₃COOH
C₆H₅-OH + R-CO-Cl → C₆H₅-O-CO-R + HCl
Conditions: Acid anhydrides or acid chlorides with base (Schotten-Baumann)

Ether Formation

Williamson Ether Synthesis

C₆H₅-O⁻Na⁺ + R-X → C₆H₅-O-R + NaX
Conditions: Phenoxide ion with alkyl halide (SN₂)

Oxidation Reactions

Formation of Quinones

C₆H₄(OH)₂ + [O] → C₆H₄O₂ + H₂O
Oxidizing agents: Chromic acid, Fremy's salt, silver oxide

Coupling Reactions

Azo Coupling

C₆H₅-OH + Ar-N₂⁺ → Ar-N=N-C₆H₄-OH + H⁺
Conditions: Diazonium salts in alkaline medium, para substitution
Important Tests for Phenols

1. Ferric chloride test: Violet/blue/green color with FeCl₃
2. Bromine water test: White precipitate of tribromophenol
3. Libermann's test: Deep blue color with NaNO₂/H₂SO₄
4. Phthalein test: Phenolphthalein formation with phthalic anhydride

Applications of Phenols

Industrial and Medical Uses

  • Antiseptics & Disinfectants: Phenol, cresols (Lysol), hexachlorophene
  • Polymers: Phenol-formaldehyde resins (Bakelite), epoxy resins
  • Explosives: Picric acid (2,4,6-trinitrophenol)
  • Drugs: Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid), paracetamol
  • Dyes: Azo dyes via diazonium coupling
  • Antioxidants: BHT (butylated hydroxytoluene) in food

Phenol-Formaldehyde Resin (Bakelite)

C₆H₅-OH + HCHO → Network polymer
Conditions: Acid or base catalyst, heat and pressure
Uses: First synthetic plastic (1907), electrical insulators, handles
Environmental and Health Concerns

Phenols are environmental pollutants from industrial waste. They are toxic to aquatic life and can cause skin irritation, respiratory issues, and systemic toxicity in humans. Proper disposal and wastewater treatment are essential.

Natural Occurrence

Phenols occur naturally in many plants: salicylic acid in willow bark, eugenol in cloves, thymol in thyme, and urushiol in poison ivy (causes allergic reactions).
