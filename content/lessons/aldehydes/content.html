Introduction to Aldehydes

Aldehydes are organic compounds containing a carbonyl group (C=O) with at least one hydrogen atom attached to the carbonyl carbon. They have the general formula R-CHO.

Definition

General formula: R-CHO where R can be hydrogen, alkyl, or aryl group.

The carbonyl carbon is sp² hybridized, trigonal planar, and electrophilic.

Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones due to less steric hindrance and the presence of the hydrogen atom, which makes them easily oxidized.

Nomenclature of Aldehydes

IUPAC Rules

1. Identify longest chain containing the -CHO group
2. Replace -e with -al in parent alkane name
3. The carbonyl carbon is always carbon #1
4. Use Greek letters (α, β, γ) for substituent positions

Examples

H-CHO → Methanal (Formaldehyde)
CH₃-CHO → Ethanal (Acetaldehyde)
CH₃CH₂CH₂-CHO → Butanal
C₆H₅-CHO → Benzaldehyde
Common Names

Many aldehydes have common names derived from their corresponding carboxylic acids: Formaldehyde, Acetaldehyde, Propionaldehyde, Benzaldehyde

Physical Properties

Polarity and Intermolecular Forces

Aldehydes are polar molecules due to the carbonyl group. They have dipole-dipole interactions but cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves (no O-H bond).

Boiling Point Comparison

  • Propane (C₃H₈): -42°C
  • Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO): 20°C
  • Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): 78°C
  • Formaldehyde (HCHO): -19°C (gas at room temp)

Lower aldehydes (C₁-C₄) are soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding with water molecules. Solubility decreases with increasing chain length.

Characteristic Odors

• Formaldehyde: Pungent, irritating smell (used in preservatives)
• Acetaldehyde: Fruity, apple-like odor
• Benzaldehyde: Bitter almond aroma (used in flavorings)
• Cinnamaldehyde: Cinnamon smell

Preparation Methods

Oxidation of Primary Alcohols

Controlled Oxidation

R-CH₂OH + [O] → R-CHO + H₂O
Conditions: PCC (Pyridinium chlorochromate) in CH₂Cl₂

PCC stops oxidation at aldehyde stage

Ozonolysis of Alkenes

Reductive Workup

R-CH=CH-R' → R-CHO + R'-CHO
Conditions: 1. O₃, CH₂Cl₂, -78°C; 2. Zn/H₂O or (CH₃)₂S

Hydration of Alkynes

Kucherov Reaction

RC≡CH + H₂O → R-CO-CH₃
Conditions: HgSO₄/H₂SO₄, H₂O, 60°C

Terminal alkynes give methyl ketones, not aldehydes

Rosenmund Reduction

Acid Chloride to Aldehyde

R-COCl + H₂ → R-CHO + HCl
Conditions: H₂, Pd/BaSO₄, quinoline (poison), 150°C

Stephen Reaction

Nitrile to Aldehyde

R-CN + SnCl₂ + HCl → R-CHO + NH₄Cl
Conditions: SnCl₂ in dry ether, HCl gas

Gattermann-Koch Reaction

Formylation of Aromatics

Ar-H + CO + HCl → Ar-CHO
Conditions: CO/HCl, CuCl/AlCl₃ catalyst, high pressure

Chemical Reactions

Oxidation Reactions

Aldehydes are easily oxidized to carboxylic acids.

Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror Test)

R-CHO + 2[Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ + 3OH⁻ → R-COO⁻ + 2Ag↓ + 4NH₃ + 2H₂O
Conditions: Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal AgNO₃), warm

Forms silver mirror on test tube - distinguishes aldehydes from ketones

Fehling's Test

R-CHO + 2Cu²⁺ + 5OH⁻ → R-COO⁻ + Cu₂O↓ + 3H₂O
Conditions: Fehling's solution (CuSO₄ + alkaline tartrate), heat

Red precipitate of Cu₂O - distinguishes aliphatic aldehydes

Reduction Reactions

Reduction to Primary Alcohols

R-CHO + 2[H] → R-CH₂OH
Reducing Agents: NaBH₄, LiAlH₄, H₂/Ni

Clemmensen Reduction

R-CHO + 4[H] → R-CH₃ + H₂O
Conditions: Zn-Hg amalgam, concentrated HCl, reflux

Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

Mechanism

Aldehydes undergo nucleophilic addition due to the electrophilic carbonyl carbon. The reaction follows: Nucleophile attacks carbonyl carbon → Tetrahedral intermediate → Protonation.

Addition of HCN (Cyanohydrin Formation)

R-CHO + HCN → R-CH(OH)CN
Conditions: Trace of base (KCN) catalyzes reaction

Addition of NaHSO₃ (Bisulfite Addition)

R-CHO + NaHSO₃ → R-CH(OH)SO₃Na
Conditions: Saturated NaHSO₃ solution

Crystalline precipitate used for purification

Addition of Grignard Reagents

R-CHO + R'-Mg-X → R-CH(R')-OMgX → R-CH(R')-OH
Conditions: Dry ether, followed by H₃O⁺ workup

Condensation Reactions

Aldol Condensation

2R-CH₂-CHO → R-CH₂-CH(OH)-CH(R)-CHO
Conditions: Dilute base (NaOH), room temperature

Cannizzaro Reaction

2H-CHO + NaOH → H-COONa + CH₃OH
Conditions: Concentrated NaOH (50%)

Only for aldehydes without α-hydrogens

Perkin Reaction

Ar-CHO + (RCH₂CO)₂O → Ar-CH=CH-COOH + RCH₂COOH
Conditions: Anhydride, sodium salt of acid, heat

Polymerization

Formaldehyde Polymerization

n H-CHO → (-CH₂-O-)ₙ
Conditions: Acid or base catalyst

Forms paraformaldehyde (n=8-100) or polyoxymethylene

Important Aldehydes

Common Aldehydes and Their Uses

  • Formaldehyde (HCHO): Disinfectant, preservative, resins (Bakelite, urea-formaldehyde)
  • Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO): Production of acetic acid, plastics, dyes, perfumes
  • Benzaldehyde (C₆H₅CHO): Food flavoring (almond extract), dyes, perfumes
  • Vanillin: Food flavoring, perfume industry
  • Cinnamaldehyde: Cinnamon flavor, antimicrobial agent
  • Glutaraldehyde: Sterilizing agent, tissue fixative
Biological Importance

• Retinal: Vision pigment (aldehyde form of vitamin A)
• Glucose: Exists as open-chain aldehyde form in equilibrium
• Pyridoxal phosphate: Coenzyme in amino acid metabolism

Qualitative Tests for Aldehydes

Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror Test)

Reagent: Ammoniacal silver nitrate [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺
Observation: Silver mirror on test tube wall
Specificity: Aldehydes only (ketones negative)

Fehling's Test

Reagent: Fehling's A (CuSO₄) + Fehling's B (alkaline tartrate)
Observation: Brick red precipitate (Cu₂O)
Specificity: Aliphatic aldehydes only

Benedict's Test

Reagent: Alkaline copper citrate solution
Observation: Green → Yellow → Red precipitate
Use: Detecting reducing sugars (glucose)

Schiff's Test

Reagent: Schiff's reagent (fuchsin dye decolorized with SO₂)
Observation: Magenta color restoration
Note: More sensitive than Tollens' or Fehling's
