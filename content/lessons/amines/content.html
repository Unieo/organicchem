Introduction to Amines

Amines are organic compounds derived from ammonia (NH₃) by replacement of one or more hydrogen atoms with alkyl or aryl groups. They are nitrogen-containing compounds with a lone pair on nitrogen.

Definition

General formulas:
• Primary: R-NH₂
• Secondary: R₂NH
• Tertiary: R₃N
• Quaternary: R₄N⁺ X⁻ (ammonium salt)

Amines are important in biological systems (amino acids, neurotransmitters), pharmaceuticals, and industrial applications. They are basic compounds that can form salts with acids.

Classification of Amines

Based on Number of Alkyl Groups

Primary, Secondary & Tertiary Amines

  • Primary (1°): One alkyl group attached to nitrogen R-NH₂ (e.g., Methylamine)
  • Secondary (2°): Two alkyl groups attached to nitrogen R₂NH (e.g., Dimethylamine)
  • Tertiary (3°): Three alkyl groups attached to nitrogen R₃N (e.g., Trimethylamine)

Based on Nature of Groups

Aliphatic vs Aromatic Amines

  • Aliphatic Amines: Alkyl groups attached to nitrogen CH₃NH₂ (more basic)
  • Aromatic Amines: Aryl groups attached to nitrogen C₆H₅NH₂ (aniline, less basic)
  • Heterocyclic Amines: Nitrogen in a ring C₅H₅N (pyridine)

Natural Occurrence

Biologically Important Amines

• Putrescine & Cadaverine: Decaying flesh smell
• Histamine: Allergic reactions, inflammation
• Serotonin & Dopamine: Neurotransmitters
• Epinephrine: Adrenaline, fight-or-flight response
• Alkaloids: Morphine, nicotine, caffeine

Nomenclature of Amines

IUPAC Rules

1. Identify longest alkyl chain containing -NH₂ group
2. Replace -e with -amine in parent alkane name
3. For secondary/tertiary amines, use N-alkyl prefix
4. Number chain to give -NH₂ group lowest possible number

Examples

CH₃NH₂ → Methanamine (Methylamine)
CH₃CH₂NH₂ → Ethanamine (Ethylamine)
CH₃NHCH₃ → N-Methylmethanamine (Dimethylamine)
C₆H₅NH₂ → Benzenamine (Aniline)
(CH₃)₃N → N,N-Dimethylmethanamine (Trimethylamine)
Common Names

Many amines are known by common names:
• Methylamine, Ethylamine, Dimethylamine
• Aniline (phenylamine)
• Pyridine, Piperidine (heterocyclic amines)

Physical Properties

Hydrogen Bonding

Primary and secondary amines can form intermolecular hydrogen bonds (N-H···N), giving them higher boiling points than comparable hydrocarbons. Tertiary amines cannot form N-H bonds, so have lower boiling points.

Boiling Point Comparison

  • Propane (C₃H₈): -42°C
  • Trimethylamine ((CH₃)₃N): 3.5°C
  • Dimethylamine ((CH₃)₂NH): 7°C
  • Methylamine (CH₃NH₂): -6.3°C
  • Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): 78°C (for comparison)
Odor and Solubility

• Odor: Lower aliphatic amines have fishy, ammonia-like smells. Cadaverine and putrescine (from decaying flesh) have extremely foul odors.
• Solubility: Lower amines (C₁-C₃) are soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding with water. Solubility decreases with increasing chain length.

Basicity of Amines

Reaction with Acids

R-NH₂ + HCl → R-NH₃⁺ Cl⁻
Result: Formation of amine hydrochloride salt (water soluble)

Basicity Order (in water)

  • Aliphatic Amines: (CH₃)₂NH > CH₃NH₂ > (CH₃)₃N > NH₃
  • Aromatic Amines: Much weaker bases than aliphatic amines
  • Reason: Electron-donating alkyl groups increase basicity; resonance in aromatic amines decreases basicity
pKa Values

• Ammonia (NH₃): pKb = 4.75
• Methylamine (CH₃NH₂): pKb = 3.36
• Dimethylamine ((CH₃)₂NH): pKb = 3.27
• Aniline (C₆H₅NH₂): pKb = 9.38
• Pyridine (C₅H₅N): pKb = 8.75

Preparation Methods

Alkylation of Ammonia

Hofmann Alkylation

NH₃ + R-X → R-NH₃⁺ X⁻ → R-NH₂
Conditions: Excess NH₃, heat
Problem: Gives mixture of 1°, 2°, 3° amines and quaternary salts

Reduction of Nitro Compounds

Nitro to Amine

R-NO₂ + 6[H] → R-NH₂ + 2H₂O
Conditions: Sn/HCl, Fe/HCl, or catalytic hydrogenation (H₂/Pd)

Specific Example: Aniline Preparation

C₆H₅-NO₂ + 3Sn + 6HCl → C₆H₅-NH₂ + 3SnCl₂ + 2H₂O
Conditions: Tin and concentrated HCl, then NaOH to free amine

Reduction of Nitriles

Nitrile to Primary Amine

R-CN + 4[H] → R-CH₂-NH₂
Reducing Agents: LiAlH₄, H₂/Ni or Pt, Na/C₂H₅OH

Reduction of Amides

Amide to Amine

R-CONH₂ + 4[H] → R-CH₂-NH₂ + H₂O
R-CONHR' + 4[H] → R-CH₂-NHR' + H₂O
Conditions: LiAlH₄ in dry ether

Hofmann Rearrangement

Amide to Amine (with loss of carbonyl)

R-CONH₂ + Br₂ + 4NaOH → R-NH₂ + Na₂CO₃ + 2NaBr + 2H₂O
Conditions: Bromine in sodium hydroxide solution

Gabriel Phthalimide Synthesis

Preparation of Pure Primary Amines

Phthalimide + KOH → Potassium phthalimide + R-X → N-Alkylphthalimide
N-Alkylphthalimide + NaOH/H₂O or NH₂NH₂ → R-NH₂ + Phthalic acid
Advantage: Produces only primary amines, no mixtures

Reductive Amination

Carbonyl to Amine

R-CHO + NH₃ + H₂ → R-CH₂-NH₂ + H₂O
R-CHO + R'-NH₂ + H₂ → R-CH₂-NH-R' + H₂O
Conditions: H₂, Ni or Pt catalyst, or NaBH₃CN

Chemical Reactions

Basic Character Reactions

Salt Formation with Acids

R-NH₂ + HCl → R-NH₃⁺ Cl⁻
Properties: Amine salts are crystalline, water-soluble, higher melting points

Reaction with Alkyl Halides

R-NH₂ + R'-X → R-NH-R' + HX
R-NH-R' + R'-X → R-N(R')₂ + HX
Note: Continues to give mixtures of amines

Acylation Reactions

Formation of Amides

R-NH₂ + R'-COCl → R-NH-COR' + HCl
R-NH₂ + (R'-CO)₂O → R-NH-COR' + R'-COOH
Conditions: Pyridine as base to remove HCl

Schotten-Baumann Reaction

C₆H₅-NH₂ + CH₃COCl + NaOH → C₆H₅-NH-COCH₃ + NaCl + H₂O
Conditions: Aqueous NaOH, room temperature

Sulfonation

Formation of Sulfonamides

R-NH₂ + Ar-SO₂Cl → R-NH-SO₂-Ar + HCl
Importance: Sulfa drugs (antibiotics), e.g., Sulfanilamide

Carbylamine Reaction

Isocyanide Formation (Primary Amines Only)

R-NH₂ + CHCl₃ + 3KOH → R-NC + 3KCl + 3H₂O
Conditions: Warm with alcoholic KOH
Test: Foul-smelling isocyanide - test for primary amines

Reaction with Nitrous Acid

Primary Aliphatic Amines

R-NH₂ + HNO₂ → R-OH + N₂↑ + H₂O
Observation: Brisk effervescence of nitrogen gas
Use: Quantitative analysis of -NH₂ groups

Primary Aromatic Amines (Diazotization)

Ar-NH₂ + NaNO₂ + 2HCl → Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + NaCl + 2H₂O
Conditions: 0-5°C (ice bath)
Importance: Diazonium salts for dye synthesis

Secondary Amines

R₂NH + HNO₂ → R₂N-NO (N-Nitrosamine)
Observation: Yellow oil (carcinogenic)

Tertiary Amines

R₃N + HNO₂ → R₃NH⁺ NO₂⁻ (salt formation)

Diazonium Salt Reactions

Importance of Diazonium Salts

Diazonium salts (Ar-N₂⁺ X⁻) are versatile intermediates for introducing various functional groups onto aromatic rings. They are prepared from primary aromatic amines at 0-5°C.

Replacement Reactions

Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + H₂O → Ar-OH + N₂↑ + HCl
Conditions: Warm water
Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + CuCl → Ar-Cl + N₂↑
Conditions: Sandmeyer reaction, CuCl catalyst
Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + CuBr → Ar-Br + N₂↑
Conditions: Gattermann reaction
Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + KI → Ar-I + N₂↑ + KCl
Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + HBF₄ → Ar-F + N₂↑ + BF₃ + HCl
Conditions: Schiemann reaction, heat
Ar-N₂⁺ Cl⁻ + CuCN → Ar-CN + N₂↑
Conditions: Sandmeyer reaction

Coupling Reactions (Azo Dye Formation)

Ar-N₂⁺ + Ar'-OH → Ar-N=N-Ar'-OH
Conditions: Weakly alkaline, orange/red dyes
Ar-N₂⁺ + Ar'-NH₂ → Ar-N=N-Ar'-NH₂
Conditions: Weakly acidic, yellow dyes

Oxidation Reactions

Oxidation of Amines

R-NH₂ + [O] → Various products
Agents: KMnO₄, H₂O₂, peracids
Products: Nitroso, nitro, or oxide compounds

Specific: Aniline Oxidation

C₆H₅-NH₂ + K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄ → C₆H₄O₂ (p-Benzoquinone)
Observation: Black precipitate (aniline black) if strong oxidation

Mustard Oil Reaction

Primary Amines with CS₂

R-NH₂ + CS₂ → R-NH-CSSH → R-NCS + H₂S
Final Product: Isothiocyanate (mustard oil)
Test: Characteristic pungent smell

Important Amines & Applications

Biologically Important Amines

  • Neurotransmitters: Dopamine, Serotonin, Norepinephrine, Acetylcholine
  • Hormones: Epinephrine (Adrenaline), Thyroxine
  • Amino Acids: Glycine, Alanine, etc. (all have -NH₂ group)
  • Alkaloids: Morphine, Codeine, Nicotine, Caffeine, Quinine
  • Vitamins: Thiamine (B₁), Niacin (B₃), Pyridoxine (B₆)
Industrial Applications

• Dyes & Pigments: Azo dyes, aniline dyes (mauveine - first synthetic dye)
• Pharmaceuticals: Sulfa drugs, antihistamines, local anesthetics (lidocaine)
• Polymers: Nylon, polyurethane, epoxy resins
• Agrochemicals: Herbicides, pesticides, fertilizers
• Solvents: Pyridine, morpholine
• Corrosion Inhibitors: Cyclohexylamine, morpholine

Example: Synthesis of Paracetamol

p-Nitrophenol → Reduction → p-Aminophenol + Acetic anhydride → Paracetamol
Importance: Common analgesic and antipyretic drug
Environmental & Safety Aspects

• Many aromatic amines are carcinogenic (benzidine, 2-naphthylamine)
• Amines can cause allergic reactions and respiratory issues
• Proper ventilation and handling procedures are essential
• Quaternary ammonium compounds are used as disinfectants and surfactants
