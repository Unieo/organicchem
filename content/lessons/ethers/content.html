Introduction to Ethers

Ethers are organic compounds containing an oxygen atom connected to two alkyl or aryl groups, with the general formula R-O-R'.

Key Feature

The C-O-C bond angle in ethers is approximately 110°, similar to water, due to sp³ hybridization of oxygen.

General Structure

R-O-R' (where R and R' can be same or different)
Symmetrical: R = R' (e.g., CH₃-O-CH₃, Dimethyl ether)
Unsymmetricall: R ≠ R' (e.g., CH₃-O-C₂H₅, Ethyl methyl ether)

Classification of Ethers

Based on Attached Groups

Types of Ethers

  • Simple/Symmetrical Ethers: Both alkyl groups same (R-O-R)
  • Mixed/Unsymmetricall Ethers: Different alkyl groups (R-O-R')
  • Aromatic Ethers (Aryl ethers): At least one aryl group (Ar-O-R or Ar-O-Ar)
  • Cyclic Ethers: Oxygen as part of a ring (e.g., epoxy, tetrahydrofuran)

Cyclic Ethers

Important Cyclic Ethers

  • Epoxides (Oxiranes): 3-membered rings (C₂H₄O)
  • Oxetanes: 4-membered rings (C₃H₆O)
  • Furan: 5-membered with double bonds
  • Tetrahydrofuran (THF): 5-membered saturated (C₄H₈O)
  • Pyran: 6-membered with double bonds
  • Tetrahydropyran: 6-membered saturated
  • Dioxane: 6-membered with two oxygens

Crown Ethers

Macrocyclic Polyethers

Large cyclic ethers containing multiple oxygen atoms that can complex with metal ions. Named as "crown-n" where n is total atoms (e.g., 18-crown-6 has 18 atoms, 6 oxygens).

Nomenclature of Ethers

Two Naming Systems

1. Common Names: Alkyl groups + "ether" (for simple ethers)
2. IUPAC Names: Alkoxy substituent on parent chain (for mixed/complex ethers)

Examples

CH₃-O-CH₃ → Dimethyl ether (common) or Methoxymethane (IUPAC)
CH₃-O-C₂H₅ → Ethyl methyl ether (common) or Methoxyethane (IUPAC)
C₂H₅-O-C₂H₅ → Diethyl ether (common) or Ethoxyethane (IUPAC)
C₆H₅-O-CH₃ → Methyl phenyl ether (common) or Methoxybenzene (IUPAC: Anisole)
CH₃-CH₂-O-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃ → Ethyl propyl ether (common) or 1-Ethoxypropane (IUPAC)
Special Names

Anisole: Methyl phenyl ether (C₆H₅-O-CH₃)
Phenetole: Ethyl phenyl ether (C₆H₅-O-C₂H₅)
Diphenyl ether: (C₆H₅)₂O

Physical Properties

Characteristic Properties

  • State: Dimethyl/ethyl methyl ethers are gases; others are volatile liquids
  • Odor: Sweet, pleasant, ethereal odor
  • Boiling Points: Similar to alkanes, lower than alcohols of comparable MW
  • Melting Points: Low due to symmetrical structure
  • Solubility: Partially soluble in water (hydrogen bonding with water)
  • Dipole Moment: Moderate (Diethyl ether: 1.18 D)
  • Density: Less than water (float on water)

Hydrogen Bonding in Ethers

Ethers cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves (no O-H bonds) but can act as hydrogen bond acceptors with water or alcohols. This explains their partial water solubility.

Important Solvent Properties

Ethers are excellent solvents for organic reactions because:
1. They dissolve both polar and nonpolar compounds
2. They are relatively inert
3. They have low boiling points for easy removal
4. They coordinate with Grignard and organolithium reagents

Preparation Methods

Williamson Ether Synthesis

Most Important Method

R-X + R'-O⁻Na⁺ → R-O-R' + NaX
Conditions: Alkoxide + alkyl halide, SN2 mechanism
Limitation: Use 1° alkyl halides to avoid elimination
Mechanism Insight

Williamson synthesis is an SN2 reaction. The alkoxide ion attacks the electrophilic carbon of the alkyl halide. Tertiary halides give elimination products (alkenes) instead.

Dehydration of Alcohols

Intermolecular Dehydration

2R-OH → R-O-R + H₂O
Conditions: Conc. H₂SO₄, 140°C (lower temp than alkene formation)
Limitation: Only gives symmetrical ethers from single alcohol

Alkoxymercuration-Demercuration

From Alkenes

CH₂=CH₂ + R-OH → CH₃-CH₂-O-R
Conditions: Hg(OAc)₂ then NaBH₄, Markovnikov addition
Advantage: No rearrangement, high yield, anti-Markovnikov

From Diazomethane

Methylation of Alcohols

R-OH + CH₂N₂ → R-O-CH₃ + N₂
Conditions: Acid catalyst (BF₃, HBF₄), very mild
Caution: Diazomethane is toxic and explosive

Ullmann Ether Synthesis

For Aryl Ethers

Ar-X + Ar'-O⁻ → Ar-O-Ar' + X⁻
Conditions: Copper catalyst, high temperature
Application: Diarylethers, electron-rich arenes work best

From Epoxides

Ring Opening with Alcohols

+ R-OH → HO-CH₂-CH₂-O-R
Conditions: Acid or base catalysis

Chemical Reactions

General Reactivity

Ethers are relatively inert compared to other oxygen-containing compounds. They resist bases, nucleophiles, and reducing agents, but react with strong acids and oxidizing agents.

Cleavage by Acids

Mechanism of Acid Cleavage

  • Step 1: Protonation of oxygen (fast)
  • Step 2: SN2 attack by halide (for 1° alkyl groups)
  • Step 3: SN1 if carbocation stable (for 3° alkyl groups)
  • Result: Two alkyl halides or alcohol + alkyl halide

With Hydrogen Halides

R-O-R' + 2HX → R-X + R'-X + H₂O
Reactivity: HI > HBr > HCl (HF doesn't react)
Temperature effect: Excess HI at high temp gives complete cleavage

With Concentrated H₂SO₄

R-O-R' + H₂SO₄ → R-OH + R'-OSO₃H
Note: Only one alkyl group cleaved, forms alkyl hydrogen sulfate

Peroxide Formation

Autoxidation Danger

R-O-R' + O₂ → R-O-O-R' (hydroperoxides and peroxides)
Conditions: Exposure to air/oxygen, light catalyzed
Danger: Peroxides are explosive when concentrated
Safety Warning

Ethers must be stored in dark bottles with antioxidants (BHT, hydroquinone). Old ether samples should be tested for peroxides before distillation (starch-iodide test gives blue color).

Complex Formation

With Lewis Acids

R₂O: + BF₃ → R₂O:→BF₃
Importance: Ethers are good Lewis bases, coordinate with Grignard reagents

Formation of Epoxides

From Alkenes via Peracids

CH₂=CH₂ + R-CO₃H → + R-COOH
Peracids: m-CPBA, peracetic acid, performic acid
Stereochemistry: Syn addition, retention of configuration

Claisen Rearrangement

Special Reaction of Allyl Aryl Ethers

CH₂=CH-CH₂-O-C₆H₅ → O-C₆H₄-CH₂-CH=CH₂
Conditions: Heat (200°C), sigmatropic [3,3] rearrangement
Product: Ortho-allyl phenol (or para if ortho occupied)

Oxidation Reactions

Formation of Hydroperoxides

CH₃-O-C₂H₅ + O₂ → CH₃-O-CH(OOH)-CH₃
Autoxidation: Occurs at α-carbon to oxygen

Comparison: Ethers vs Alcohols

Crown Ether Complexation

Host-Guest Chemistry

Crown ether + M⁺ → [Crown·M]⁺ complex
Selectivity: 12-crown-4 prefers Li⁺, 15-crown-5 prefers Na⁺, 18-crown-6 prefers K⁺
Application: Solubilize inorganic salts in organic solvents, phase transfer catalysis

Applications of Ethers

Industrial and Laboratory Uses

  • Solvents: Diethyl ether (extractions), THF (polymerization), dioxane (aqueous mixtures)
  • Anesthetics: Diethyl ether (first surgical anesthetic, 1846), enflurane, isoflurane
  • Starting Materials: For Williamson synthesis, Grignard reactions
  • Fuel Additives: MTBE (methyl tert-butyl ether) - oxygenate, now phased out
  • Protecting Groups: Methoxy groups in organic synthesis
  • Phase Transfer Catalysts: Crown ethers for solubility enhancement
  • Polymer Industry: THF for spandex, PTMEG production

Diethyl Ether as Anesthetic

First demonstrated by William T.G. Morton in 1846
Advantages: Wide safety margin, muscle relaxation
Disadvantages: Flammable, nausea, slow induction/recovery
Modern Ether Anesthetics

Modern halogenated ethers are safer: Enflurane, Isoflurane, Sevoflurane, Desflurane. They are non-flammable, have better control, and fewer side effects than diethyl ether.

MTBE Controversy

Methyl tert-butyl ether was widely used as gasoline oxygenate (reduces CO emissions) but banned in many areas due to groundwater contamination. It's highly soluble, moves quickly in water, and has unpleasant taste/odor at very low concentrations.

THF in Polymer Industry

THF → Polymerization → PTMEG → Spandex fibers
Process: Cationic ring-opening polymerization with Lewis acid catalysts
Product: Poly(tetramethylene ether) glycol (PTMEG) for elastic fibers

Safety Considerations

  • Flammability: Low flash points (diethyl ether: -45°C)
  • Peroxide Formation: Test old samples before use/distillation
  • Explosion Risk: Peroxide crystals can detonate on disturbance
  • Toxicity: Chronic exposure affects CNS, some are carcinogenic
  • Storage: Dark bottles, antioxidants, away from light/heat
  • Disposal: As hazardous organic waste, never down drain

Crown Ethers in Nobel Prize Chemistry

Charles J. Pedersen (1967) → Discovery of crown ethers → Nobel Prize 1987
Impact: Founded host-guest chemistry and supramolecular chemistry
Applications: Ion transport, molecular recognition, catalysis
