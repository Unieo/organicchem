Introduction to Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are organic compounds containing the carboxyl functional group (-COOH). They have the general formula R-COOH, where R can be hydrogen, alkyl, or aryl groups.

Definition

General formula: R-COOH where R = H, alkyl, or aryl.

The carboxyl group consists of a carbonyl (C=O) and hydroxyl (OH) group. Resonance between these groups makes carboxylic acids more acidic than alcohols.

Carboxylic acids are among the most important organic compounds in nature and industry. They occur in fats (fatty acids), proteins (amino acids), and many natural products.

Key Features

• Resonance stabilization of conjugate base
• Hydrogen bonding capability (dimers in pure state)
• Higher boiling points than comparable alcohols
• Wide range of biological and industrial importance

Classification and Nomenclature

IUPAC Rules

1. Identify longest chain containing the -COOH group
2. Replace -e with -oic acid in parent alkane name
3. The carboxyl carbon is always carbon #1
4. For dicarboxylic acids, use -dioic acid suffix

Examples

H-COOH → Methanoic acid (Formic acid)
CH₃-COOH → Ethanoic acid (Acetic acid)
CH₃CH₂CH₂-COOH → Butanoic acid (Butyric acid)
C₆H₅-COOH → Benzoic acid
HOOC-COOH → Oxalic acid (Ethanedioic acid)

Classification

Based on R Group

  • Aliphatic: Alkyl chain attached CH₃COOH (acetic acid)
  • Aromatic: Aryl group attached C₆H₅COOH (benzoic acid)
  • Fatty acids: Long aliphatic chains C₁₇H₃₅COOH (stearic acid)

Based on Number of -COOH Groups

  • Monocarboxylic: One -COOH group (acetic acid)
  • Dicarboxylic: Two -COOH groups (oxalic acid)
  • Tricarboxylic: Three -COOH groups (citric acid)
  • Polycarboxylic: Multiple -COOH groups
Common Names

Many carboxylic acids are known by common names:
• Formic acid (HCOOH, from ants)
• Acetic acid (CH₃COOH, vinegar)
• Butyric acid (C₃H₇COOH, rancid butter)
• Stearic acid (C₁₇H₃₅COOH, animal fat)
• Oleic acid (C₁₇H₃₃COOH, olive oil)

Physical Properties

Hydrogen Bonding and Dimerization

Carboxylic acids form strong intermolecular hydrogen bonds. In pure state and nonpolar solvents, they exist as dimers held together by two hydrogen bonds, doubling their effective molecular weight.

Boiling Point Comparison

  • Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): 78°C
  • Ethanal (CH₃CHO): 20°C
  • Acetic acid (CH₃COOH): 118°C
  • Propanoic acid (C₂H₅COOH): 141°C
  • Butanoic acid (C₃H₇COOH): 164°C
Solubility and Odor

• Lower acids (C₁-C₄) are completely miscible with water
• Solubility decreases with increasing chain length
• C₁-C₄ acids have pungent, irritating odors
• C₅-C₉ acids have unpleasant odors (goaty smell)
• C₁₀+ acids are essentially odorless

Acidity of Carboxylic Acids

Dissociation in Water

R-COOH + H₂O ⇌ R-COO⁻ + H₃O⁺
Acidity Constant: Ka = [R-COO⁻][H₃O⁺]/[R-COOH]
pKa Range: 3-5 for most carboxylic acids

pKa Values of Common Acids

  • Formic acid: 3.75
  • Acetic acid: 4.76
  • Chloroacetic acid: 2.86
  • Dichloroacetic acid: 1.48
  • Trichloroacetic acid: 0.70
  • Benzoic acid: 4.20
  • Oxalic acid: 1.25 (first dissociation)
Factors Affecting Acidity

1. Inductive effect: Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity
2. Resonance stabilization: Of conjugate base
3. Solvation: Better solvation of conjugate base increases acidity
4. Steric effects: Can hinder solvation of conjugate base

Spectral Properties

Characteristic IR Absorption

O-H stretch: 2500-3300 cm⁻¹ (broad)
C=O stretch: 1700-1725 cm⁻¹ (strong)
C-O stretch: 1210-1320 cm⁻¹
¹³C NMR Chemical Shifts

Carbonyl carbon: δ 165-185 ppm
α-carbon: δ 20-50 ppm
Carboxyl carbon in salts: δ 170-190 ppm (slightly downfield)

Preparation Methods

Oxidation of Primary Alcohols

Complete Oxidation

R-CH₂OH + 2[O] → R-COOH + H₂O
Oxidizing Agents: KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄, HNO₃, CrO₃

Oxidation of Alkenes

Oxidative Cleavage

R-CH=CH-R' → R-COOH + R'-COOH
Conditions: Hot KMnO₄ or ozonolysis with oxidative workup

Hydrolysis of Nitriles

Acidic or Basic Hydrolysis

R-CN + 2H₂O → R-COOH + NH₃
Acidic: Conc. HCl or H₂SO₄, reflux
Basic: NaOH, then acidification
Note: Adds one carbon to chain

Grignard with CO₂

Carbonation of Grignard Reagents

R-Mg-X + CO₂ → R-COO-Mg-X → R-COOH
Conditions: Dry ether, solid CO₂ (dry ice), then acidic workup

Malonic Ester Synthesis

Alkylation and Hydrolysis

CH₂(COOC₂H₅)₂ → Alkylation → Hydrolysis → Decarboxylation → R-CH₂-COOH
Conditions: NaOC₂H₅, R-X, then hydrolysis and heat

Hydrolysis of Trihalides

Haloform Reaction (Methyl Ketones)

R-CO-CH₃ + 3X₂ + 4NaOH → R-COONa + CHX₃ + 3NaX + 3H₂O
Conditions: X₂ in NaOH, known as haloform reaction

Kolbe-Schmitt Reaction

Hydroxybenzoic Acids from Phenol

C₆H₅ONa + CO₂ → o-HO-C₆H₄-COONa
Conditions: Sodium phenoxide, CO₂ (4-7 atm), 125°C

Main product is salicylic acid (ortho), some para isomer

Oxidation of Alkylbenzenes

Side Chain Oxidation

Ar-CH₃ + 3[O] → Ar-COOH + H₂O
Conditions: KMnO₄/OH⁻, heat or CrO₃/H₂SO₄

Any alkyl group with α-hydrogen oxidizes to -COOH

Hydrolysis of Acid Derivatives

From Esters, Amides, Anhydrides

R-COOR' + H₂O → R-COOH + R'-OH
R-CONH₂ + H₂O → R-COOH + NH₃
(R-CO)₂O + H₂O → 2R-COOH
Conditions: Acidic or basic hydrolysis

Chemical Reactions

Reactivity Pattern

Carboxylic acids undergo reactions at three main sites: 1) The acidic proton (salt formation), 2) The hydroxyl group (esterification, etc.), 3) The carbonyl carbon (reduction, nucleophilic substitution).

Salt Formation

Reaction with Bases

R-COOH + NaOH → R-COONa + H₂O
R-COOH + NaHCO₃ → R-COONa + CO₂ + H₂O
Test: Effervescence with NaHCO₃ distinguishes acids from phenols

Esterification

Fischer Esterification

R-COOH + R'-OH ⇌ R-COOR' + H₂O
Conditions: Acid catalyst (H₂SO₄, HCl), heat, reversible

Mechanism

Protonation → Nucleophilic attack → Proton transfer → Elimination
Rate: Primary alcohols > Secondary > Tertiary
Le Chatelier: Remove water or use excess alcohol to drive equilibrium

Formation of Acid Chlorides

Reaction with Thionyl Chloride

R-COOH + SOCl₂ → R-COCl + SO₂ + HCl
Conditions: SOCl₂ (best reagent), PCl₃, PCl₅
Advantage: Gaseous byproducts easy to remove

Formation of Anhydrides

From Acid Chlorides

R-COCl + R'-COO⁻ → R-CO-O-CO-R' + Cl⁻
Conditions: Acid chloride + carboxylate salt, pyridine

Cyclic Anhydrides

o-C₆H₄(COOH)₂ → C₆H₄(CO)₂O + H₂O
Conditions: Heat (succinic, phthalic acids form anhydrides easily)

Formation of Amides

Direct Reaction (Difficult)

R-COOH + R'-NH₂ → R-CONHR' + H₂O
Conditions: Heat, reversible, poor yield
Better: Via acid chloride or ester

Reduction Reactions

Reduction to Primary Alcohols

R-COOH + 4[H] → R-CH₂OH + H₂O
Reducing Agents: LiAlH₄ (best), B₂H₆ (borane)
Note: NaBH₄ does NOT reduce carboxylic acids

Reduction to Aldehydes (Rosemund Reduction)

R-COCl + H₂ → R-CHO + HCl
Conditions: H₂, Pd/BaSO₄, quinoline poison, 150°C
Specific: Only from acid chlorides, stops at aldehyde

Decarboxylation Reactions

Loss of CO₂

R-COOH → R-H + CO₂
Conditions: Heat with soda lime (NaOH/CaO)
Easier for: β-keto acids, α-nitro acids, malonic acids

Kolbe Electrolysis

2R-COO⁻ → R-R + 2CO₂ + 2e⁻
Conditions: Electrolysis of sodium carboxylate, anode reaction

Halogenation (Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky)

α-Halogenation of Carboxylic Acids

R-CH₂-COOH + X₂ → R-CHX-COOH + HX
Conditions: Red P or PX₃ catalyst, X₂ (Br₂ or Cl₂)
Mechanism: Forms acid bromide in situ, enolizes, halogenates

Aromatic Substitution

Electrophilic Substitution

Benzoic acid + HNO₃/H₂SO₄ → m-Nitrobenzoic acid
Directing effect: -COOH is meta-directing, deactivating
Reactions: Nitration, sulfonation, halogenation (all meta)

Special Reactions

Arndt-Eistert Synthesis

R-COOH → R-CH₂-COOH (homologation)
Steps: Acid chloride → diazoketone → Wolff rearrangement

Schmidt Reaction

R-COOH + HN₃ → R-NH₂ + CO₂ + N₂
Conditions: Hydrazoic acid, concentrated H₂SO₄
Product: Amine with one less carbon

Hunsdiecker Reaction

R-COOAg + Br₂ → R-Br + CO₂ + AgBr
Conditions: Silver salt, bromine, CCl₄, heat or light
Product: Alkyl bromide with one less carbon

Derivatives of Carboxylic Acids

Reactivity Order

Acid chlorides > Anhydrides > Esters ≈ Acids > Amides
More reactive derivatives can be converted to less reactive ones

Acid Chlorides

Properties and Reactions

R-COCl + H₂O → R-COOH + HCl (hydrolysis)
R-COCl + R'-OH → R-COOR' + HCl (esterification)
R-COCl + 2R'-NH₂ → R-CONHR' + R'-NH₃⁺ Cl⁻ (amide formation)
R-COCl + R'-COONa → (R-CO)₂O + NaCl (anhydride formation)
Characteristics: Fuming liquids, pungent odor, moisture sensitive

Acid Anhydrides

Properties and Reactions

(R-CO)₂O + H₂O → 2R-COOH (hydrolysis)
(R-CO)₂O + R'-OH → R-COOR' + R-COOH (alcoholysis)
(R-CO)₂O + R'-NH₂ → R-CONHR' + R-COOH (aminolysis)
Characteristics: Less reactive than acid chlorides, used in industry

Esters

Properties and Reactions

R-COOR' + H₂O ⇌ R-COOH + R'-OH (hydrolysis)
R-COOR' + R"-OH ⇌ R-COOR" + R'-OH (transesterification)
R-COOR' + NH₃ → R-CONH₂ + R'-OH (ammonolysis)
R-COOR' + R"-Mg-X → R-C(R")(OH)-R' (Grignard addition)
Characteristics: Pleasant odors (fruity), used in flavors and fragrances

Amides

Properties and Reactions

R-CONH₂ + H₂O → R-COOH + NH₃ (hydrolysis)
R-CONH₂ + HNO₂ → R-COOH + N₂ + H₂O (with nitrous acid)
R-CONH₂ + Br₂/NaOH → R-NH₂ (Hofmann rearrangement)
R-CONH₂ + LiAlH₄ → R-CH₂-NH₂ (reduction)
Characteristics: High melting solids, hydrogen bonding, least reactive

Interconversion of Derivatives

Pathways

Carboxylic Acid → Acid Chloride (SOCl₂) → Anhydride (R'COO⁻) → Ester (R"OH) → Amide (NH₃)
Hydrolysis converts all derivatives back to carboxylic acids

Important Carboxylic Acids

Common Carboxylic Acids and Uses

  • Formic acid (HCOOH): Textile dyeing, leather tanning, preservative, found in ant venom
  • Acetic acid (CH₃COOH): Vinegar (5%), industrial chemical (glacial acetic acid), solvent, precursor to vinyl acetate
  • Citric acid (C₆H₈O₇): Food additive, antioxidant, cleaning agent, found in citrus fruits
  • Lactic acid (CH₃CHOHCOOH): Food preservation, cosmetics, biodegradable plastics, produced in muscles during exercise
  • Oxalic acid (HOOC-COOH): Rust remover, bleaching agent, found in spinach and rhubarb
  • Benzoic acid (C₆H₅COOH): Food preservative, precursor to phenol, found naturally in berries
  • Stearic acid (C₁₇H₃₅COOH): Soap manufacture, candles, found in animal fats
  • Oleic acid (C₁₇H₃₃COOH): Main component of olive oil, soap manufacture, monounsaturated fatty acid
  • Acetylsalicylic acid (Aspirin): Pain reliever, anti-inflammatory, antipyretic
Biological Importance

• Amino acids: Building blocks of proteins (20 standard amino acids)
• Fatty acids: Components of fats and membranes (saturated and unsaturated)
• Krebs cycle intermediates: Citric acid, α-ketoglutaric acid, succinic acid, fumaric acid, malic acid, oxaloacetic acid
• DNA/RNA: Nucleic acids contain phosphate-carboxylic ester linkages
• Vitamins: Ascorbic acid (vitamin C), pantothenic acid (B₅)
• Hormones: Prostaglandins, leukotrienes

Industrial Applications

• Polymers: Polyesters (PET, Dacron), polyamides (nylon), acrylics
• Pharmaceuticals: Aspirin, penicillin, ibuprofen, many antibiotics
• Food industry: Preservatives, acidulants, flavorings
• Soaps and detergents: Sodium/potassium salts of fatty acids
• Solvents: Acetic acid, formic acid
• Agriculture: Herbicides (2,4-D), plant growth regulators

Fatty Acids

Classification

  • Saturated: No double bonds (palmitic, stearic acids)
  • Monounsaturated: One double bond (oleic acid)
  • Polyunsaturated: Multiple double bonds (linoleic, linolenic acids)
  • Essential fatty acids: Must be obtained from diet (linoleic, linolenic)
Omega Classification

Based on position of first double bond from methyl end:
• Omega-3: First double bond at carbon 3 (fish oils, flaxseed)
• Omega-6: First double bond at carbon 6 (vegetable oils)
• Omega-9: First double bond at carbon 9 (olive oil)

Tests and Identification

Tests for Carboxylic Acids

Sodium Bicarbonate Test

Reagent: NaHCO₃ solution
Positive: Effervescence (CO₂ gas)
Specificity: Distinguishes carboxylic acids from phenols

Litmus Test

Reagent: Blue litmus paper
Positive: Turns red
Note: Strong enough to change litmus color

Ester Test

Reagent: Alcohol + conc. H₂SO₄
Positive: Fruity odor of ester
Procedure: Warm acid with alcohol and few drops of conc. H₂SO₄

Tests for Derivatives

Hydroxamic Acid Test (Esters)

Reagent: NH₂OH·HCl + NaOH, then FeCl₃
Positive: Violet/red color with Fe³⁺
Specific for: Esters, acid chlorides, anhydrides

Hinsberg Test (Amides vs Amines)

Reagent: C₆H₅SO₂Cl + KOH
Amides: No reaction or hydrolysis
Amines: Form sulfonamides with different solubility

Spectral Identification

Characteristic Spectral Data

• IR: Broad O-H (2500-3300 cm⁻¹), strong C=O (1700-1725 cm⁻¹)
• ¹H NMR: Carboxylic proton δ 10-13 ppm (very downfield)
• ¹³C NMR: Carbonyl carbon δ 165-185 ppm
• MS: Often shows M-17 peak (loss of OH), M-45 peak (loss of COOH)

Distinction from Other Acidic Compounds

vs Phenols: Carboxylic acids give CO₂ with NaHCO₃, phenols do not
vs Enols: Carboxylic acids are stronger acids
vs Sulfonic acids: Sulfonic acids are much stronger (pKa ~ -1 to -2)
