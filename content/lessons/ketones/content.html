Introduction to Ketones

Ketones are organic compounds containing a carbonyl group (C=O) bonded to two carbon atoms. They have the general formula R-CO-R', where R and R' can be alkyl or aryl groups.

Definition

General formula: R-CO-R' where R and R' are alkyl/aryl groups (same or different).

The carbonyl carbon is sp² hybridized, trigonal planar, and electrophilic. Unlike aldehydes, ketones have no hydrogen attached to the carbonyl carbon.

Ketones are important industrial solvents and chemical intermediates. They occur naturally in many sugars (ketoses) and as hormones (testosterone, progesterone).

Key Difference from Aldehydes

Ketones are less reactive than aldehydes due to:
1. Steric hindrance from two alkyl groups
2. Electron-donating effect of alkyl groups reduces carbonyl electrophilicity
3. Cannot be oxidized by mild oxidizing agents

Nomenclature of Ketones

IUPAC Rules

1. Identify longest chain containing the carbonyl group
2. Replace -e with -one in parent alkane name
3. Number chain to give carbonyl carbon lowest possible number
4. Indicate position with locant before -one suffix

Examples

CH₃-CO-CH₃ → Propanone (Acetone)
CH₃-CO-CH₂CH₃ → Butan-2-one (Methyl ethyl ketone)
CH₃CH₂-CO-CH₂CH₃ → Pentan-3-one (Diethyl ketone)
C₆H₅-CO-CH₃ → Phenylethanone (Acetophenone)
C₆H₅-CO-C₆H₅ → Diphenylmethanone (Benzophenone)
Common Names

Many ketones have common names derived from the alkyl groups:
• Acetone (simplest ketone)
• Methyl ethyl ketone (MEK)
• Acetophenone (methyl phenyl ketone)
• Benzophenone (diphenyl ketone)

Special Naming Rules

For Complex Ketones

  • When -CHO and -CO- both present, aldehyde takes priority (-al suffix)
  • Use oxo- prefix for carbonyl as substituent in compounds with higher priority groups
  • Cyclic ketones: Use cycloalkanone (e.g., cyclopentanone)
  • Diketones: Add -dione suffix (e.g., butane-2,3-dione)

Physical Properties

Polarity and Intermolecular Forces

Ketones are polar molecules due to the carbonyl group. They have dipole-dipole interactions but cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves (no O-H bond).

Boiling Point Comparison

  • Butane (C₄H₁₀): -1°C
  • Diethyl ether ((C₂H₅)₂O): 34.6°C
  • Acetone (CH₃COCH₃): 56°C
  • Butan-2-one (CH₃COC₂H₅): 80°C
  • Butan-1-ol (C₄H₉OH): 118°C (for comparison)
Solubility and Miscibility

• Lower ketones (C₃-C₅) are soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding with water molecules
• Acetone is completely miscible with water
• Solubility decreases with increasing chain length
• Excellent solvents for organic compounds

Spectral Properties

Characteristic IR Absorption

C=O stretch: 1705-1725 cm⁻¹ (strong, sharp)
Note: Slightly lower than aldehydes (1720-1740 cm⁻¹)
Use: Easy identification in infrared spectroscopy
¹³C NMR Chemical Shifts

Carbonyl carbon appears at δ 190-220 ppm (downfield)
α-carbons appear at δ 30-50 ppm
Useful for structure determination

Preparation Methods

Oxidation of Secondary Alcohols

Using Strong Oxidizing Agents

R-CH(OH)-R' + [O] → R-CO-R' + H₂O
Oxidizing Agents: K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄, KMnO₄, CrO₃, Na₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄

Ketones resist further oxidation under mild conditions

Hydration of Alkynes

Kucherov Reaction (Terminal Alkynes)

RC≡CH + H₂O → R-CO-CH₃
Conditions: HgSO₄/H₂SO₄ catalyst, H₂O, 60°C

Terminal alkynes give methyl ketones exclusively via enol intermediate

Internal Alkynes

R-C≡C-R' + H₂O → R-CO-CH₂-R' (or mixture)
Conditions: HgSO₄/H₂SO₄, Markovnikov addition

Friedel-Crafts Acylation

Aromatic Ketone Synthesis

Ar-H + R-CO-Cl → Ar-CO-R + HCl
Conditions: AlCl₃ catalyst (Lewis acid), dry solvent (CS₂, CH₂Cl₂)

No rearrangement occurs (unlike Friedel-Crafts alkylation)

Ozonolysis of Alkenes

Cleavage of Double Bonds

R-CH=CH-R' → R-CHO + R'-CHO (or ketones if tetrasubstituted)
Conditions: 1. O₃, CH₂Cl₂, -78°C; 2. Zn/H₂O or (CH₃)₂S

Weinreb Amide Method

Controlled Addition of Grignard Reagents

R-CO-N(OMe)CH₃ + R'-Mg-X → R-CO-R'
Conditions: Weinreb amide, Grignard reagent, then acidic workup

Prevents over-addition to give tertiary alcohols

Dakin Reaction

o-Hydroxyaryl Aldehydes to Phenols

o-HO-C₆H₄-CHO + H₂O₂ → o-HO-C₆H₄-OH
Conditions: Alkaline H₂O₂

Hydrolysis of Geminal Dihalides

Vicinal Dihalides to Ketones

R-CHX-CHX-R' + 2KOH → R-CO-R' + 2KX + 2H₂O
Conditions: Aqueous KOH, heat

Wacker Process

Industrial Acetaldehyde Production

CH₂=CH₂ + ½O₂ → CH₃CHO
Conditions: PdCl₂/CuCl₂ catalyst, aqueous medium, 100°C, 10 atm

Chemical Reactions

Reactivity Pattern

Ketones undergo nucleophilic addition reactions at the carbonyl carbon. Reactivity order: Formaldehyde > Aldehydes > Ketones. Steric and electronic factors make ketones less reactive than aldehydes.

Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

Addition of Grignard Reagents

R-CO-R' + R"-Mg-X → R-C(R")(OH)-R' → R-C(R")(OH)-R' after hydrolysis
Conditions: Dry ether, then H₃O⁺ workup
Product: Tertiary alcohol

Addition of HCN (Cyanohydrin Formation)

R-CO-R' + HCN → R-C(CN)(OH)-R'
Conditions: Trace of base (KCN) catalyzes reaction
Equilibrium: Favored for aldehydes, less for ketones

Addition of NaHSO₃ (Bisulfite Addition)

R-CO-R' + NaHSO₃ → R-C(SO₃Na)(OH)-R'
Conditions: Saturated NaHSO₃ solution
Note: Works only for methyl ketones and cyclic ketones

Reduction Reactions

Reduction to Secondary Alcohols

R-CO-R' + 2[H] → R-CH(OH)-R'
Reducing Agents: NaBH₄, LiAlH₄, H₂/Ni, Na/C₂H₅OH

Clemmensen Reduction

R-CO-R' + 4[H] → R-CH₂-R' + H₂O
Conditions: Zn-Hg amalgam, concentrated HCl, reflux
Product: Alkane (methylene group)

Wolff-Kishner Reduction

R-CO-R' + NH₂NH₂ → R-CH₂-R' + N₂ + H₂O
Conditions: Hydrazine, KOH, ethylene glycol, 200°C
Note: Basic conditions alternative to Clemmensen

MPV Reduction (Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley)

R-CO-R' + (CH₃CH₂O)₃Al → R-CH(OH)-R' + CH₃CHO
Conditions: Aluminum isopropoxide, isopropanol, reflux
Specificity: Selective reduction of ketones over aldehydes

Oxidation Reactions

Baeyer-Villiger Oxidation

R-CO-R' + RCOOOH → R-COO-R' + RCOOH
Conditions: Peroxyacid (mCPBA, peracetic acid), acid catalyst
Migration order: Tertiary alkyl > cyclohexyl > secondary > phenyl > primary > methyl

Haloform Reaction (Methyl Ketones)

CH₃-CO-R + 3X₂ + 4NaOH → CHX₃ + R-COONa + 3NaX + 3H₂O
Conditions: X₂ (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) in NaOH, warm
Test: Iodoform test - yellow precipitate of CHI₃

Condensation Reactions

Aldol Condensation (with α-hydrogens)

2 CH₃-CO-CH₃ → (CH₃)₂C(OH)CH₂COCH₃
Conditions: Dilute base (NaOH) or acid catalyst
Product: β-hydroxy ketone (aldol)

Claisen-Schmidt Condensation

Ar-CHO + CH₃-CO-Ar → Ar-CH=CH-CO-Ar + H₂O
Conditions: Alkali, cross-aldol between aldehyde and ketone

Knoevenagel Condensation

R-CHO + CH₂(COOC₂H₅)₂ → R-CH=C(COOC₂H₅)₂ + H₂O
Conditions: Weak base (piperidine), malonic ester derivatives

Benzoin Condensation

2 Ar-CHO → Ar-CH(OH)-CO-Ar
Conditions: CN⁻ catalyst (nucleophilic acylation)
Note: Only for aromatic aldehydes

Enolization and Tautomerism

Keto-Enol Tautomerism

Ketones exist in equilibrium with their enol forms. For simple ketones, keto form is greatly favored (>99%). Enol content increases with: α-carbonyl group stabilization, conjugation, intramolecular H-bonding.

Acidity of α-Hydrogens

R-CO-CH₂-R' + Base → R-CO-CH-R' (enolate ion)
pKa: ~20 for ketones (weaker acid than alcohols but stronger than alkynes)
Importance: Enolate ions are key intermediates in many reactions

Halogenation Reactions

Acid-Catalyzed α-Halogenation

R-CO-CH₃ + X₂ → R-CO-CH₂X + HX
Conditions: Acid catalyst (H⁺), X₂ (Cl₂, Br₂)
Mechanism: Enol formation then electrophilic attack

Base-Catalyzed α-Halogenation

R-CO-CH₃ + 3X₂ + 3NaOH → R-CO-CX₃ + 3NaX + 3H₂O
Conditions: Excess halogen, base
Result: Trihalomethyl ketone → Haloform reaction

Special Reactions

Wittig Reaction

R-CO-R' + Ph₃P=CHR" → R-C(R")=CH-R' + Ph₃P=O
Conditions: Wittig reagent (ylide), mild conditions
Use: Alkene synthesis from carbonyls

Reformatsky Reaction

R-CO-R' + Br-CH₂COOC₂H₅ + Zn → R-C(OH)(CH₂COOC₂H₅)-R'
Conditions: Zinc metal, dry ether, then hydrolysis
Product: β-hydroxy ester

Important Ketones

Common Ketones and Their Uses

  • Acetone (CH₃COCH₃): Common solvent, nail polish remover, precursor to methyl methacrylate (plexiglass)
  • Methyl ethyl ketone (MEK, CH₃COC₂H₅): Industrial solvent for resins, lacquers, paints
  • Cyclohexanone: Nylon production (precursor to adipic acid and caprolactam)
  • Acetophenone (C₆H₅COCH₃): Fragrance industry, precursor to resins
  • Benzophenone (C₆H₅COC₆H₅): UV absorber, fragrance fixative, photoinitiator
  • Methyl isobutyl ketone (MIBK): Solvent for paints, varnishes, extraction processes
  • Muscone (3-methylcyclopentadecanone): Musk fragrance from musk deer
Biological Importance

• Ketone bodies: Acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone - produced during starvation/fasting
• Steroid hormones: Testosterone, progesterone, cortisone contain ketone groups
• Sugars: Fructose (ketose sugar)
• Ubiquinone: Electron carrier in respiration (also called coenzyme Q)
• Prostaglandins: Many contain ketone groups

Industrial Applications

• Solvents for paints, varnishes, adhesives
• Pharmaceutical intermediates
• Polymer production (PMMA from acetone)
• Nylon production from cyclohexanone
• Fragrance and flavor industry
• Photoinitiators in UV-curable inks and coatings

Tests and Identification

Distinction from Aldehydes

Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror Test)

Reagent: Tollens' reagent [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺
Aldehydes: Positive - silver mirror
Ketones: Negative - no reaction (except α-hydroxyketones)

Fehling's Test

Reagent: Fehling's solution (Cu²⁺ tartrate complex)
Aldehydes: Positive - red Cu₂O precipitate
Ketones: Negative - no reaction

Schiff's Test

Reagent: Schiff's reagent (fuchsin dye + SO₂)
Aldehydes: Positive - magenta color
Ketones: Negative - no color change (slow reaction)

Specific Tests for Ketones

Iodoform Test (Methyl Ketones)

Reagent: I₂ in NaOH solution
Positive: Yellow precipitate of CHI₃ (iodoform)
Specific for: CH₃-CO- or CH₃CH(OH)- groups

2,4-DNP Test (Dinitrophenylhydrazine)

Reagent: 2,4-Dinitrophenylhydrazine in acidified ethanol
Positive: Yellow/orange/red precipitate (hydrazone)
Specificity: General test for carbonyl compounds

Sodium Nitroprusside Test

Reagent: Sodium nitroprusside + NaOH
Positive: Red color for ketones with α-CH₂ group
Note: Aldehydes give no color or different color
Spectral Identification

• IR: Strong C=O stretch at 1705-1725 cm⁻¹
• ¹H NMR: α-protons at δ 2.0-2.5 ppm
• ¹³C NMR: Carbonyl carbon at δ 190-220 ppm
• MS: α-cleavage characteristic fragmentation pattern
